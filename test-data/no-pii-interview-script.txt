So there was this one afternoon last week. I was working from a small cafe near my apartment, and everything just worked. I had my laptop, a strong coffee, and about two hours before I had to be somewhere. And in that window I got more done than in the entire previous week sitting at my desk at home.

A few things made it click, I think. The background noise. At home it's too quiet, and I start noticing every small interruption, every notification, every thought. The cafe gave me just enough ambient sound to settle. Also, I had no other tabs open. I had decided on the way over that I was only going to work on one document. No email, no messages, no chat. Just write.

The constraint helped a lot. The two-hour window was real. I could not pretend I had all day. So I started writing immediately. No warm-up, no procrastination loop. Just open the file and go.

The days that do not work, the pattern is the same every time. I sit down at my desk in the morning. I open my laptop. There are already seven things demanding attention. Chat notifications, email, the task list from yesterday with things I did not finish. I tell myself I will just check one thing. Forty minutes later I am deep in a thread about something I do not care about, and I have not even opened the thing I was supposed to be working on.

By the end of those days I feel exhausted but not in a good way. Like I have been pushed around by other people's priorities. I have been busy without producing anything. And the next morning I sit down and the same task is still waiting, and now I am a day behind, and I feel worse.

If I could change one thing, it would be the morning. The first hour. Right now the first hour is reactive. I open everything, I respond to whatever is loudest. If I could protect the first hour, just one hour where nothing else gets touched, I think the rest of the day would follow. The cafe afternoons work because they are effectively a protected first hour, just shifted to the afternoon. They are a workaround for not being able to protect the morning at home.

The honest thing is that I know what would help, and I still do not do it. So maybe the real question is why I do not do the obvious thing. But that is a different interview.

I tried a timer once. Twenty-five minutes of work, five minutes of break. It worked for two days and then I lost it. Not because the method was wrong. Because I started using the break to check messages, and then the next twenty-five-minute block was already broken before it started. The break was supposed to be rest. It became more input.

Weekends are different. On a weekend, the laptop stays closed in the morning. I make coffee. I sit by the window. I read for half an hour before anything starts. By the time I open the laptop, my head is already mine. On weekdays, the laptop is open before I am awake. The day belongs to it before I get a chance.

The pattern is not really about productivity. It is about who owns the first decision of the day. Right now, my inbox owns it. And I keep telling myself that next week will be different.
